Chapter 12.2 — The Language of Probability

Learning the notation so we can talk about uncertainty precisely

Part 1: What Are Random Variables?

Think of it like a form at the dentist's office

When you go to the dentist, they might track several things about you. Each of these is a random variable — something whose value we might not know yet.

Cavity
Does the patient have a cavity?
Values: true or false (Boolean)
Toothache
Does the patient have a toothache?
Values: true or false (Boolean)
Catch
Does the dentist's probe catch?
Values: true or false (Boolean)
Weather
What's the weather today?
Values: sunny, rain, cloud, snow (multi-valued)

Convention: We use uppercase for the variable name (Cavity) and lowercase for a specific value (cavity = true, ¬cavity = false). This seems fussy but it matters when reading formulas.

Part 2: Three Types of Probability Statements

P(cavity) = 0.2
Prior / Unconditional probability
Before we know ANYTHING else: 20% of patients have a cavity. This is what you'd guess walking in blind.
P(cavity | toothache) = 0.6
Conditional / Posterior probability
GIVEN that we know the patient has a toothache: now there's a 60% chance of cavity. New evidence updated our belief.

Analogy: Weather forecasting

Prior: "There's a 30% chance of rain tomorrow" (before checking anything).
Posterior: "Given that the barometer is dropping, there's a 70% chance of rain" (after seeing evidence).

Evidence updates our beliefs. That's the whole point of conditional probability.

The bold P notation — distributions

Sometimes we want ALL the probabilities at once. The textbook uses bold P for this:

A distribution gives you all values at once:
P(Cavity) = ⟨0.2, 0.8⟩
This means P(cavity) = 0.2 and P(¬cavity) = 0.8 — two numbers that add up to 1.

Part 3: The Rules of Probability (Axioms)

There are really just three basic rules. Everything else follows from these:

1
0 ≤ P(a) ≤ 1
Every probability is between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain). Nothing more, nothing less.
2
P(true) = 1   and   P(false) = 0
Things that are definitely true have probability 1. Things that are definitely false have probability 0.
3
P(a ∨ b) = P(a) + P(b) − P(a ∧ b)
"The probability of A or B" = P(A) + P(B) minus the overlap (where both are true). If they can't both be true, just add them.

Quick example: Axiom 3 in action

→
What's the probability a patient has a cavity OR a toothache (or both)?
→
P(cavity) = 0.2, P(toothache) = 0.2, and P(cavity ∧ toothache) = 0.12
→
P(cavity ∨ toothache) = 0.2 + 0.2 − 0.12 = 0.28
→
We subtract 0.12 because those patients have BOTH — we don't want to count them twice.
Important consequence: P(¬a) = 1 − P(a). If there's a 20% chance of cavity, there's an 80% chance of no cavity. They must add up to 1.

Part 4: The Product Rule and Bayes' Rule

These two rules are the most important formulas in the entire chapter.

The Product Rule — how to compute joint probabilities:
P(a ∧ b) = P(a | b) · P(b)
"The chance of both A and B" = "the chance of A given B" × "the chance of B"

Analogy: Two doors

To get to room B, you have to first open door A (probability 0.3) and then door B only opens if A is open (probability 0.5 given A is open).

Chance of reaching room B = 0.3 × 0.5 = 0.15.

Bayes' Rule — THE formula of this chapter:
P(b | a) = P(a | b) · P(b) / P(a)
"Flip" a conditional probability. If you know P(a|b), you can compute P(b|a).

Bayes' Rule: The Meningitis Example

A doctor knows these facts:

→
P(stiff neck | meningitis) = 0.7 — meningitis causes a stiff neck 70% of the time
→
P(meningitis) = 1/50,000 — meningitis is very rare
→
P(stiff neck) = 0.01 — 1% of all patients have a stiff neck

Question: A patient has a stiff neck. How likely is meningitis?

→
P(meningitis | stiff neck) = 0.7 × (1/50000) / 0.01 = 0.0014
→
Only 0.14%! Even though meningitis USUALLY causes a stiff neck, stiff necks are mostly caused by other things. The disease is just too rare.

Why Bayes' Rule matters for AI

In the real world, doctors/dentists/agents usually know things in the causal direction:

P(symptom | disease) — "this disease causes this symptom X% of the time"

But what they NEED is the diagnostic direction:

P(disease | symptom) — "given these symptoms, what disease does the patient have?"

Bayes' Rule lets you flip from cause→effect to effect→cause. That's why it's the foundation of medical AI, spam filters, and almost all probabilistic reasoning.

Section 12.2 — What You Need to Remember

  • Random variables represent things we don't know yet (Cavity, Toothache, etc.)
  • Prior P(a) = belief before seeing evidence. Posterior P(a|b) = belief after evidence.
  • Three axioms — probabilities are between 0 and 1, and the "or" rule handles overlaps.
  • Product rule: P(a ∧ b) = P(a|b) · P(b) — how to compute "both happen"
  • Bayes' rule: P(b|a) = P(a|b)·P(b)/P(a) — flip from cause→effect to effect→cause
  • Causal vs. diagnostic: We usually know cause→effect. Bayes lets us go effect→cause.
