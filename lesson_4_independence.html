Chapter 12.4: Independence

Welcome to Chapter 12.4! In the previous sections, you learned about probability and how to represent uncertainty using joint probability distributions. You discovered that a joint distribution over n variables can have exponentially many entries — 2^n entries for binary variables.

But here's a powerful insight: not all variables truly influence each other. Some variables are independent — knowing about one tells you nothing about the other. When we recognize this independence, we can dramatically reduce the amount of information needed to specify our probabilities.

This section is about finding and exploiting that independence. Let's begin!

1. What is Independence? The Expanded Dentist Scenario

Recall the dentist example from earlier chapters. You have three variables:

  • Cavity: Do you have a cavity? (True/False)
  • Toothache: Do you have a toothache? (True/False)
  • Catch: Does the dentist's probe catch? (True/False)

The joint probability distribution P(Cavity, Toothache, Catch) has 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 entries. That's manageable.

Now let's expand the scenario. You're sitting in the waiting room, and you notice a fourth variable:

  • Weather: What's the weather outside? (Sunny, Rainy, Cloudy, or Snowy)

With four variables, the joint distribution P(Cavity, Toothache, Catch, Weather) has 2 × 2 × 2 × 4 = 32 entries.

But ask yourself: Does the weather affect whether you have a cavity?

Intuition: The weather doesn't care about your teeth! Whether it's sunny, rainy, cloudy, or snowing outside has absolutely no bearing on cavity formation, toothaches, or whether the probe catches. These are completely separate domains. Your cavity probability is the same on a sunny day as on a snowy day.

This is the essence of independence. Two variables are independent if knowing the value of one variable doesn't give us any new information about the other.

2. Formal Definition of Independence

The Core Idea

Two variables A and B are independent if any of these three equivalent statements hold:

Definition 1: Conditional Probability Version

P(A | B) = P(A)

This says: "The probability of A given B is the same as the probability of A without any information about B." Knowing B doesn't change your belief about A.

Example: P(Cavity | Weather = Sunny) = P(Cavity). Learning the weather is sunny doesn't make cavities more or less likely.

Definition 2: The Symmetric Version

P(B | A) = P(B)

Similarly, knowing A doesn't change your belief about B.

Example: P(Weather = Sunny | Cavity) = P(Weather = Sunny). Knowing whether you have a cavity doesn't change the weather distribution!

Definition 3: The Joint Distribution Version

P(A ∧ B) = P(A) · P(B)

This is the most mathematically elegant form. The joint probability factors into a product of individual probabilities.

Example: P(Cavity ∧ Weather) = P(Cavity) × P(Weather)

This captures independence perfectly: there's no interaction between the variables in the joint distribution.

Why three definitions? They're mathematically equivalent — all three say the same thing in different ways. Definition 1 and 2 emphasize the intuition: knowing one variable doesn't change your beliefs about the other. Definition 3 is what we use for computation and reasoning, because the factorization allows us to break apart complex probability tables.

3. The Payoff: Factoring Reduces Computation

Now comes the payoff for recognizing independence. Let's return to the expanded dentist scenario:

Without recognizing independence:

Full Joint Distribution

P(Cavity, Toothache, Catch, Weather)

32 entries

We must specify the probability for all 32 combinations.

With recognizing independence:

Since Weather is independent of the dental variables, we can factor the joint:

P(Cavity, Toothache, Catch, Weather) = P(Cavity, Toothache, Catch) × P(Weather)

Now we only need to specify:

  • P(Cavity, Toothache, Catch): 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 entries
  • P(Weather): 4 entries (one for each weather type)
  • Total: 8 + 4 = 12 entries

Factored Representation

P(Cavity, Toothache, Catch) × P(Weather)

12 entries

We broke it into smaller pieces.

Without Independence

Must store all combinations

32

Memory: O(2^4)

With Independence

Factor into separate parts

12

Memory: O(2^3 + 4)

That's a 62.5% reduction in storage! And this savings grows exponentially as we add more independent variables.

If we had 10 independent variables, we'd go from 2^10 = 1,024 entries to just 10 entries. That's the power of independence.

Think of it like a recipe: You could write one giant table of all possible combinations of ingredients and cooking methods. But if some ingredients are truly independent (salt doesn't affect your choice of flour), you can write a smaller recipe that lists them separately. The full recipe can be reconstructed by "multiplying" the independent parts together.

4. A Cleaner Example: Independent Coin Flips

Let's use a simpler, cleaner example to solidify the concept of independence. Imagine you flip a fair coin n times. Each flip is independent of every other flip — the coin has no memory!

Let C₁, C₂, ..., Cₙ be the outcomes of the n flips (each is Heads or Tails).

The Joint Probability Without Independence

If we didn't know about independence, we'd specify the full joint distribution P(C₁, C₂, ..., Cₙ) with 2^n entries.

For just 10 flips: 2^10 = 1,024 entries. For 20 flips: 2^20 = 1,048,576 entries. This explodes!

The Joint Probability With Independence

But coin flips are independent! Each flip doesn't affect any other. So:

P(C₁, C₂, ..., Cₙ) = P(C₁) × P(C₂) × ... × P(Cₙ)

For a fair coin, P(Cᵢ = Heads) = 0.5 and P(Cᵢ = Tails) = 0.5 for all i.

So we only need to specify n probabilities — one for each variable!

  • 10 flips: 10 probabilities instead of 1,024 entries
  • 20 flips: 20 probabilities instead of 1,048,576 entries
  • 100 flips: 100 probabilities instead of 2^100 ≈ 10^30 entries

Why This Works Mathematically

By the chain rule of probability:

P(C₁, C₂, ..., Cₙ) = P(C₁) · P(C₂|C₁) · P(C₃|C₁,C₂) · ... · P(Cₙ|C₁,...,Cₙ₋₁)

But if each flip is independent, then:

  • P(C₂|C₁) = P(C₂) — knowing C₁ doesn't help predict C₂
  • P(C₃|C₁,C₂) = P(C₃) — knowing the first two flips doesn't help predict C₃
  • And so on...

All the conditional probabilities reduce to unconditional ones, so we get:

P(C₁, C₂, ..., Cₙ) = P(C₁) × P(C₂) × P(C₃) × ... × P(Cₙ)
Independence in action: Think of coin flips like independent dice rolls. Each die doesn't "know" what the others rolled. If you want to calculate the probability of a specific sequence of rolls, you just multiply the individual probabilities. The dice don't conspire to influence each other.

5. The Limitation: Absolute Independence is Rare

Important Reality Check: Complete independence between variables is RARE in real-world problems. Most variables have some relationship to each other.

Let's return to the original dentist problem. We have three dental variables:

  • Cavity
  • Toothache
  • Catch

Are Toothache and Catch independent?

No! They are NOT independent.

Why are they dependent? Both variables are related to the underlying cause: whether you have a cavity. Think of it this way:
  • If the dentist's probe catches, it suggests you might have a cavity
  • If you might have a cavity, you're more likely to have a toothache
  • Therefore, knowing the probe catches makes toothache more likely

The two symptoms are "connected" through the hidden cause (the cavity).

Formally, Toothache and Catch are NOT independent because:

P(Toothache | Catch) ≠ P(Toothache)

Knowing the probe catches changes your belief about whether you have a toothache.

The Real Structure

In reality, the dental scenario has this structure:

  • Cavity is the underlying cause (a hidden variable)
  • Toothache and Catch are symptoms (effects) of Cavity
  • Toothache and Catch are NOT independent, but they would be independent if we knew the true value of Cavity

This leads to a more subtle and powerful concept: conditional independence, which is the topic of the next section.

Preview: Conditional Independence

Two variables A and B are conditionally independent given C if:

P(A | B, C) = P(A | C)

In the dental scenario: P(Toothache | Catch, Cavity) = P(Toothache | Cavity)

This says: "If you know whether there's a cavity, then knowing whether the probe catches doesn't tell you anything new about the toothache." The cavity is the key variable that connects them.

We'll explore this powerful concept in depth in the next section!

Lesson learned: Don't assume independence unless you have a good reason (like physically independent coin flips). Most real-world variables have hidden dependencies. The key is finding the structure of those dependencies — which is where conditional independence comes in.

Summary: Key Takeaways

What is Independence?

Two variables A and B are independent if knowing one tells you nothing new about the other. Formally: P(A|B) = P(A), or equivalently, P(A ∧ B) = P(A) · P(B).

Why It Matters

Independence allows us to factor large joint probability distributions into smaller, independent pieces. This exponentially reduces storage and computation.

The Payoff

n independent binary variables go from 2^n entries to just n probabilities. With 100 variables, that's 10^30 entries down to 100!

The Reality Check

Absolute independence is rare. Most real-world variables are dependent through hidden causes. We need conditional independence to capture these subtle relationships.

Looking Ahead

Now that you understand independence, you're ready for the next big idea: conditional independence. This is where independence becomes truly powerful in AI and probability reasoning. Conditional independence allows us to build efficient probabilistic models even when variables are directly dependent, as long as we condition on the right variables.

This is the foundation of Bayesian networks — one of the most important tools in modern AI!
